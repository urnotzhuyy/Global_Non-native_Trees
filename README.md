Global pattern and drivers of tree introductions

Trees are introduced outside their native ranges, including globally threatened species, where they can pose conservation risks or benefits. A key question is understanding the global extent and drivers of non-native tree introductions. Using over 30-million occurrence records of 50,470 tree species, we find that 26,096 (51.7%) were introduced as non-natives and 167 (63.7%) families of the whole tree species harbor threatened non-native species, with introductions across all and globally threatened species phylogenetically conserved. South America, North America, then Temperate Asia had the highest richness of non-natives, while Europe had the highest proportion (88.4% of total richness), with intracontinental movement dominating introductions. Taller species, those with lower leaf phosphorus per mass and higher specific leaf area are more frequently introduced, with increasing non-native richness associated with warmer, wetter climates and higher social development, which pattern of drivers deviate from the one for native species. The prevalence of early successional and competitive traits underscores the risk of future potential invasion, urging policies that prioritize native biodiversity.
